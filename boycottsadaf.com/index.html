Six Reasons to Boycott Sadaf

Incentivized Trade to Promote Normalization:

The State Department’s USAID office lists Sadaf as a viable trade partner in the Arab world. Sadaf exchanges goods from Qualified Industrial Zones (QIZs), which were formed by the United States to promote economic  normalization

Sadaf Supports Settler Colonialism:

Sadaf has expressed support for “israel” and participated in events that directly benefit the “israeli” occupation forces.

Sadaf Does Direct Business with “israel:”

Sadaf transacts with settlers as a registered business in the colony. It collaborates with zionist agricultural collectives, merchants, processing centers, and shipment logistic company, Zim.

Sadaf coopts & appropriates indigenous culture:

Sadaf appropriates regional cuisine and exploits our food culture for profit, using Arabic names and script while forging “Israeli” recipes. They normalize their zionist presence on our shelves by appearing familiar.

Sadaf is funded by zionist banks:

Sadaf is a client of Israel Discount Bank, which services businesses within “israel”. This bank was created shortly after al-Nakba to support settlers and their industries.

Sadaf Got Caught Using Harmful Ingredients:

Sadaf has used harmful ingredients in the spices our community unfortunately consumes. In 2017, Sadaf was found to have violated Proposition 65, “The Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986 in California, where it is based.

Food and Cultural Appropriation

How Sadaf Disguises Itself as Arab?

Common in stores that carry “Middle Eastern” groceries, Sadaf is a “Mizrahi” (مستعربين) operation that shrouds itself in our food and culture. Mizrahim forge a false indigenous identity as a “Middle eastern heritage” to promote such things as “israeli” falafel or hummus.

Sadaf (owned by Soofer Inc.) steal Arab culture and deceive us into thinking it is a brand for us through its packaging, choice of product lines, and selling in Arab groceries.

Sadaf takes advantage of its proximity to Arab culture and identity, then culturally appropriates our ingredients and recipes — they are not part of our community.
